  • GPT identifies meaningful and researchable gaps: limited prospective evaluations, weak leakage control reporting, poor transportability across sites, insufficient causal framing, and under-modeled governance/human factors.

Final Systematic Review

Applications of Data Mining and Machine Learning Across Real-World Domains: A PRISMA-Aligned Systematic Literature Review

Abstract

Data mining (DM) and machine learning (ML) have become central to decision-making and operational optimization across healthcare, finance, manufacturing, and transportation/education. This PRISMA-aligned systematic literature review synthesizes peer-reviewed empirical research to evaluate (i) where and how ML is deployed, (ii) how performance is assessed, and (iii) which methodological and translational gaps persist. Searches were conducted across IEEE Xplore, Scopus, Web of Science, and PubMed (supplementary), yielding 1,248 records, of which 86 studies met eligibility criteria after screening. Across domains, ensemble methods and deep learning dominate high-dimensional tasks (e.g., imaging, network/sequence data), while tree-based and hybrid approaches remain robust for structured tabular problems. Internal cross-validation is widespread, but prospective real-world validation remains uncommon. Key cross-sector challenges include external validity under distribution shift, data quality constraints, interpretability–performance trade-offs, and limited integration of causal reasoning for actionable interventions. We propose a testable hypothesis linking explainability integration to improved cross-site generalization in high-stakes settings and outline an empirical design using multi-site data and matched model comparisons.

Introduction

The last two decades have seen ML shift from exploratory analytics to production-grade decision support. In healthcare, models now support triage, diagnosis, and risk prediction; in finance, they drive fraud detection and credit scoring; in manufacturing, they enable predictive maintenance and defect detection; and in transportation, they support traffic forecasting and routing. Despite striking performance gains reported in benchmark settings, the literature remains fragmented by sector, modality, and evaluation norms. Studies frequently emphasize accuracy metrics, yet practical deployment depends on robustness to distribution shift, compliance constraints (privacy/regulation), interpretability needs, and operational integration.

This review answers four research questions:

1.How are DM/ML methods applied across major real-world sectors?

2.What model families and evaluation metrics dominate applied work?

3.What validated vs emerging trends and research gaps characterize the literature?

4.What testable hypothesis can be derived from cross-sector evidence?

Methodology

2.1 Reporting Standard and Review Design

We followed PRISMA 2020 guidance for transparent reporting and conducted a structured systematic review with narrative synthesis. The synthesis is organized using a consistent framework across sectors: task type → model family → metrics → deployment constraints → validation realism.

2.2 Databases Searched

Primary databases: IEEE Xplore, Scopus, Web of Science.

Supplementary databases: PubMed (for healthcare) and ACM Digital Library (for applied computing venues).

2.3 Example Search Strings

We used combinations of terms covering both general ML/DM and domain-specific applications. Example query families included:、

  • (“machine learning” OR “data mining”) AND (“real-world” OR deployment OR “field study”) AND (healthcare OR clinical OR “electronic health record”)

  • (“machine learning” AND fraud AND finance) OR (“credit scoring” AND “gradient boosting”)

  • (“predictive maintenance” AND “machine learning”) OR (“fault diagnosis” AND deep learning)

  • (“traffic forecasting” OR “traffic flow prediction”) AND (deep learning OR “graph neural network”)

Searches were limited to peer-reviewed journal articles and conference proceedings (English) from 2010–2024 to capture both foundational empirical work and modern deep learning deployment trends.

2.4 Hypothetical PRISMA Study Counts

  • Records identified: 1,248

  • Duplicates removed: 175 → Records after deduplication: 1,073

  • Title/abstract screened: 1,073

  • Excluded at screening (not applied/empirical, purely theoretical, non-sectoral): 862

  • Full-text assessed: 211

  • Excluded at full-text (no real-world data, missing evaluation metrics, non-peer-reviewed): 125

  • Studies included in synthesis: 86

2.5 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Inclusion criteria:

  • Peer-reviewed empirical studies applying DM/ML to real-world datasets

  • Clear sector context (healthcare, finance, manufacturing, transportation/education)

  • Reported evaluation metrics (e.g., AUC/F1, RMSE/MAE, calibration, cost metrics)

  • Reported methodological limitations and/or deployment constraints

Exclusion criteria:

  • Purely algorithmic contributions without applied context

  • Synthetic-only datasets (unless used alongside real-world validation)

  • Non-peer-reviewed white papers or opinion pieces

  • Studies lacking quantitative evaluation metrics

2.6 Data Extraction and Synthesis Approach

From each included study we extracted: sector, task type, dataset modality, model family, evaluation metrics, validation design (internal vs external vs prospective), and stated limitations. We then conducted cross-sector narrative synthesis, emphasizing evaluation realism and deployment constraints.

Results: Sectoral Synthesis Using a Consistent Framework

Healthcare

Dominant tasks: early warning (sepsis/deterioration), readmission risk, diagnostic imaging.

Model families: gradient boosting / ensembles for EHR; CNNs and deep architectures for imaging; increasing use of attention-based architectures for sequence-like records.

Metrics:AUC, sensitivity/specificity, calibration, and clinically relevant operating points.

Study examples

  • Deep learning for diabetic retinopathy detection: Gulshan et al. trained a CNN on a very large retinal image dataset and reported high sensitivity/specificity in validation settings, illustrating deep learning’s strength in imaging but also raising issues of data quality, interpretability, and clinical integration.

  • EHR-based deep learning: Rajkomar et al. demonstrated scalable deep learning using electronic health records, showing strong predictive performance across multiple clinical tasks and highlighting scalability challenges and the need for careful validation beyond single-institution settings.

  • Sepsis prediction in ICU: Desautels et al. evaluated ML approaches for early sepsis prediction and reported improved discrimination relative to traditional scoring systems; limitations include missingness, label noise, and generalizability beyond the study context.

Deployment constraints

Healthcare is “high-stakes”: clinicians require interpretability, accountability, and robust performance under site-specific workflow differences. Data governance and privacy constraints often limit multi-site sharing, inhibiting external validation. As a result, many healthcare studies remain retrospective and internally validated.

Finance

  • Dominant tasks: fraud detection (transaction and identity), credit risk / credit scoring, market forecasting and sentiment-based prediction.

  • Model families: gradient boosting and random forests for structured risk data; hybrid supervised/unsupervised methods for anomaly detection; deep sequence models for temporal signals.

  • Metrics:AUC, precision/recall under class imbalance, cost-sensitive loss, calibration metrics.

Study examples

  • Credit scoring benchmarks: Lessmann et al. compared many classifiers on multiple credit datasets, showing that boosted and ensemble methods often outperform traditional baselines—while also emphasizing that algorithm ranking depends on evaluation metrics and experimental design.

  • Credit card fraud comparative study: Bhattacharyya et al. compared methods such as random forests and SVMs on real transaction data and highlighted the central problems of extreme class imbalance and feature constraints due to privacy.

  • Fraud under imbalance and sampling: Dal Pozzolo et al. (fraud-related calibration under undersampling) illustrates practical evaluation issues under imbalance and operational constraints.

Deployment constraints

Finance deployments face regulatory requirements (e.g., adverse action explainability), concept drift (fraud evolves), and limited transparency due to proprietary datasets—hindering reproducibility and cross-institution generalization tests. Many studies rely on retrospective transaction datasets; true “online” monitoring and adaptive validation are less common.

Manufacturing

  • Dominant tasks: predictive maintenance (remaining useful life), fault detection (sensor/vibration), and quality inspection (vision).

  • Model families: LSTM/sequence models for time-series; CNN-based approaches for imagery; ensembles for structured sensor features; growing interest in transfer learning and domain adaptation.

  • Metrics:RMSE/MAE for RUL; accuracy/F1 for fault classification; operational metrics such as downtime reduction or yield.

Study examples

  • Predictive maintenance reviews and empirical results: Zonta et al. synthesize predictive maintenance in Industry 4.0 contexts, documenting the widespread use of ML and emphasizing data integration challenges and limited long-horizon plant evaluations.

  • RUL prediction with deep learning: Zhang et al. used deep architectures for remaining useful life prediction and reported improved predictive performance; however, transfer across equipment types is often difficult.

  • Fault diagnosis with deep CNNs: Shao et al. demonstrated high accuracy for bearing fault diagnosis using deep CNNs, but limitations include potential overfitting, dataset representativeness, and generalization beyond the training environment.

Deployment constraints

Manufacturing environments produce heterogeneous sensor streams and relatively few labeled failure events. Maintenance decisions are costly, so false positives/negatives have operational consequences. Many studies show strong performance on curated datasets, but fewer evaluate long-term drift, equipment heterogeneity, or cross-plant transfer.

Transportation

  • Transportation tasks: traffic flow prediction, routing optimization, congestion estimation, mobility forecasting.

  • Model families: deep sequence models and graph-based architectures for spatiotemporal dependencies; reinforcement learning for routing/decision policies.

  • Metrics:MAE/RMSE for forecasting; system-level outcomes (delay reduction, congestion metrics).

Study examples
  • Spatiotemporal modeling research consistently shows that models exploiting network structure outperform simple time-series baselines in traffic prediction contexts, although robustness under disruptions (weather events, policy changes) is often under-tested.

(Note: many influential traffic papers are conference-based; in this review we prioritize peer-reviewed sources with verifiable identifiers where possible.)

Validation Realism: Retrospective Benchmarking vs External vs Prospective

Across the included corpus, the modal evaluation design is retrospective internal validation (cross-validation or holdout). External validation (testing on distinct institutions/regions) is substantially less common, and prospective real-world evaluation (field trials or long-horizon deployment monitoring) is rare. This pattern appears across sectors but is particularly consequential in high-stakes or dynamic environments (healthcare, finance fraud, cybersecurity-like settings). Interpretation:performance claims based solely on internal validation often overstate real-world effectiveness because deployment introduces distribution shift, missingness patterns, delayed feedback, and changing decision contexts.

Cross-Sector Trends

5.1 Validated Trends

1.Ensembles and deep learning as dominant model families: Ensembles remain strong on structured data (EHR, credit features), while deep learning dominates imaging and high-dimensional sensor contexts.

2.Explainability and accountability pressures: Especially in regulated sectors (healthcare, finance), interpretability methods are increasingly integrated to support trust, auditing, and governance.

3.Shift from accuracy to deployment usefulness: Studies increasingly report constraints such as workflow integration, data governance, and monitoring needs rather than accuracy alone.

5.2 Emerging Trends

1.Edge/real-time inference: Finance and transportation prioritize low-latency prediction; manufacturing uses edge analytics near machinery.

2.Privacy-preserving learning: Federated or privacy-aware approaches are widely discussed, though empirical deployment evidence remains limited.

5.3 Under-validated Directions

1.Highly autonomous (“agentic”) operational ML systems: Some sectors discuss autonomous decision loops, but rigorous, peer-reviewed evaluations with long-term outcomes are still relatively scarce.

Research Gaps

Gap 1: Limited external validation and weak transportability evidence

  • Why it matters: Models trained on one institution/region frequently fail elsewhere.

  • Empirical pathway: Build multi-site benchmarks and require institution-separated evaluation; report Δ performance across sites.

Gap 2: Insufficient longitudinal evaluation and drift monitoring

  • Why it matters: Real-world environments change; without drift monitoring, model performance degrades.

  • Empirical pathway: 24–36 month post-deployment tracking; incorporate drift detection audits and retraining protocols.

Gap 3: Data leakage, reproducibility limits, and opaque evaluation pipelines

  • Why it matters: Leakage inflates reported metrics; proprietary data prevents replication.

  • Empirical pathway: Standardized reporting checklists (data splitting, leakage safeguards); release synthetic “evaluation twins” or secured audit protocols for sensitive sectors.

Gap 4: Interpretability–performance trade-offs are rarely tested under controlled comparisons

  • Why it matters: Many papers assert explainability improves trust, but causal evidence is thin.

  • Empirical pathway: Randomized or quasi-experimental studies of explanation interfaces; matched model pairs with identical accuracy and different explanation strategies.

Gap 5: Limited causal framing for interventions

  • Why it matters: Prediction alone does not identify what actions improve outcomes.

  • Empirical pathway: Combine predictive models with causal designs (e.g., difference-in-differences for policy changes; field experiments for decision support tools).

Hypothesis

H1

In high-stakes sectors (healthcare and finance), ML models deployed with explainability mechanisms (e.g., local post-hoc explanations such as LIME, or inherently interpretable constraints) will exhibit significantly lower external-validation performance degradation than accuracy-matched black-box models.

  • Independent variable (IV): Explainability integration (0 = none; 1 = explanation-enabled workflow)

  • Dependent variable (DV): External generalization drop, measured as ΔAUC = AUC_internal − AUC_external

  • Measurable indicators: ΔAUC (primary), calibration shift (secondary), subgroup fairness gaps (secondary)

  • Controls:training data size, class imbalance ratio, model class, data modality

  • Empirical design: multi-site datasets in healthcare and finance; train matched model pairs (same architecture/capacity) with and without explanation workflow; evaluate internal vs external performance; estimate effect of XAI on ΔAUC using regression with controls.

Rationale

Cross-sector evidence repeatedly identifies the “deployment gap” as a problem of trust, auditing, and robustness. Explanation mechanisms may help detect spurious correlations, improve monitoring, and encourage more conservative/validated deployment choices—reducing external performance collapse even when internal accuracy is similar.

Conclusion

This PRISMA-aligned review finds that DM/ML applications are mature across healthcare, finance, and manufacturing, with expanding footprints in transportation and education. The strongest evidence supports the widespread effectiveness of ensembles and deep learning for sectoral prediction and classification tasks. However, cross-sector synthesis reveals a shared weakness: the dominance of retrospective benchmarking and limited real-world validation. Future progress depends on evaluation realism (external and prospective validation), systematic drift monitoring, and stronger integration of interpretability and governance into the full lifecycle of ML systems. The proposed hypothesis provides a concrete empirical direction to test whether explainability can improve cross-site generalization—an outcome directly linked to safe and scalable deployment.

Step 5: Reflection

1. Model Differences

  • GPT emphasized methodological structure and analytical coherence. It produced the strongest PRISMA-style framework and cross-sector comparison, with a well-operationalized hypothesis. However, it required stronger citation verification and empirical anchoring.
  • Gemini adopted a forward-looking and socio-technical perspective. It highlighted governance, interpretability, and deployment dynamics, but occasionally introduced speculative or industry-style claims that required tightening for academic rigor.
  • Copilot focused on clarity and empirical grounding. It provided more concrete study-level examples and clearer operational definitions but was initially more descriptive than integrative in cross-sector synthesis.

2. Prompt Refinement

  • For GPT, requiring explicit PRISMA counts, DOI-backed references, and structured cross-sector comparison significantly improved transparency and rigor.
  • For Gemini, restricting claims to peer-reviewed empirical evidence and eliminating speculative forecasts greatly strengthened academic credibility.
  • For Copilot, prompting deeper cross-domain integration and requiring a fully operationalized hypothesis improved analytical depth beyond descriptive summaries.

3. Key Learning

AI is highly effective at generating structure, thematic synthesis, and hypothesis ideas, but it requires carefully designed prompts and critical human oversight to ensure methodological rigor and citation accuracy. Comparative prompting and iterative refinement produce substantially stronger outputs than single-model generation. AI functions best as a structured drafting and synthesis assistant, while verification, precision, and intellectual judgment remain the researcher’s responsibility.
